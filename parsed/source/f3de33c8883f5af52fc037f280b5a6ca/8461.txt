SUBJECT:     PRESIDENT'S INITIATIVE ON RACE WEEKLY REPORT
             JUNE 20 - JUNE 26


ADVISORY BOARD ACTIVITIES

      Greater Columbia Community Relations Council. On June 23, Dr. John Hope
      Franklin delivered the keynote address at the Greater Columbia Community Relations
      Council's 34th Annual Luncheon in Columbia, South Carolina. Dr. Franklin discussed
      the Initiative, its accomplishments and challenges to an audience of over 700 participants
      from various ethnic, social and religious backgrounds.

      AU-American Cities Awards. On June 20 and 21, Governor Winter chaired the panel
      of judges for the 1998 All-American Cities Awards of the National Civic League, as he
      has done for the past three years. He indicated that this year's applicants demonstrated
      an increased focus on issues of diversity and inclusion.

     Hubert Humphrey Commemoration. Governor William Winter spoke at the June 23 -
     24 commemoration of the 50th Anniversary of Hubert Humphrey's speech on civil rights
     to the Democratic National Convention. The event was hosted by the Hubert Humphrey
     Institute at the University of Minnesota, in St. Paul, Minnesota. Other participants
     included Julian Bond, Chairman of the Board of Directors of the NAACP, and Richard
     Rodriguez, editor at the Pacific News Service and essayist for The NewsHour with Jim
     Lehrer.

      Hispanic Corporate Council. On June 24, Angela Oh spoke about the Initiative to an
      audience of 100 corporate executives.

      Meeting with the Writers Guild. On June 25, Angela Oh held a roundtable dialogue

OFFICE OF THE EXECUTIVE DIRECTOR

    Race and Health Meeting. The Department of Health and Human Services is
    sponsoring a meeting on July lOin Boston, Massachusetts on issues relating to race and
    health. Dr. David Satcher, Assistant Secretary for Health and Surgeon General of the
    United States, will be the keynote speaker. The goal of the meeting is to convene experts
    in the field of health care and community leaders to identify ways of eliminating the
    disparities in health care that affect racial and ethnic minorities.

    Corporate Leaders Forum. On July 23, Advisory Board member Bob Thomas will
    host the next Corporate Leaders Forum in St. Louis. This forum will consist of a panel
    discussion of best practices in the workplace. It also will include breakout sessions on
    the application of best practices to individual workplace situations and networking
    between majority- and women and minority-owned businesses.

    Religious Leaders Meeting. On June 22, we convened a meeting of religious and civil
    rights leaders to discuss possible ways in which to continue outreach efforts to the
    religious community after September 30, 1998. Among the topics discussed were the
    ways the clergy could invoke moral authority to promote racial reconciliation, efforts to
    increase public awareness and understanding of racial issues, and the creation of a
    National Day of Focus on Racial Reconciliation.

    Washington National Cathedral Meeting. On June 23, we met with representatives of
    the Washington National Cathedral to assist them in the development of a program
    designed to promote racial reconciliation. We also discussed ideas for the utilization of a
    documentary by one of our promising practices, City at Peace, in their proposed program.

FEDERAL AGENCY ACTIVITIES

    Department of Commerce

    Federal Benchmarks for Minority Contracting. The Department of Commerce
    released new rules setting the federal benchmarks in identifying those industries that still
    suffer the effects of racial discrimination. In these industries, price credits will be given
    to small disadvantaged businesses in comparing low bids for Federal contracts.

Department ofInterior

Indian Gaming Meeting. On June 23, Secretary Babbitt presided over a meeting of
representatives of Indian Tribes, State Governors, Attorneys General and DOJ. The
topic was whether common ground was possible on the impasse in the compact
negotiation process to the Indian Gaming Regulatory Act (IGRA) created by the Supreme
Court's decision in the Seminole case. The Seminole decision held unconstitutional the
IGRA's provision that allowed tribes to sue states for failure to bargain in good faith over
gaming compacts. The parties agreed further discussions might be useful with the goal
of consensus amendments to the Act for submission to Congress, probably early next
year.
